# Universal Claims Ledger
## Consolidated Claims from the QV33R Research Corpus

---

## Methodology

This ledger consolidates claims from 9 individual author deep research reports (Rimbaud, Wilde, Burroughs, Forster, Cavafy, Baldwin, Genet, Delany, Bishop) and 1 comparative report. The original corpus contained approximately 210 raw claims. After deduplication --- merging claims that appear in multiple reports with different wording into single entries --- the ledger contains 175 entries.

**Cross-Validation Status definitions:**
- **unique**: Only one author report makes this claim
- **corroborated**: 2+ reports provide independent evidence for the same claim
- **contested**: Reports provide conflicting evidence about this claim

**Confidence scoring:**
- 90--100: Corroborated across 3+ reports with strong evidence
- 70--89: Corroborated by 2 reports or strongly supported by 1
- 50--69: Plausible but single-source or weak evidence
- Below 50: Speculative, contested, or narrow applicability

## Summary

### Key Themes

**1. Concealment as Engine, Not Obstacle (Claims 4, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12)**
The most consistent finding across the corpus is that concealment is not merely defensive but generative. The pressure to encode produces formal innovations --- pronoun drift, negative space, character distribution, genre modulation --- that would not exist without the constraint. This is documented across all 9 authors and is the single most robust finding of the research corpus.

**2. The Audience Split Is Universal (Claims 1, 2, 15, 147)**
Every studied author writes for at least two audiences simultaneously. The mechanism varies (shared knowledge, social vocabulary, structural literacy), but the split itself is present in all cases. This is the core structural feature of the double-channel text.

**3. Self-Censorship as Composition (Claims 12, 13, 14, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20)**
The decision about what not to publish is as compositionally significant as the decision about what to publish. This is documented with archival evidence in Rimbaud (silence), Wilde (revision history), Burroughs (33-year suppression), Forster (suppression of *Maurice*), Cavafy (dual publication), Bishop (exclusion of direct poems), and Genet (self-censored editions).

**4. Legal and Institutional Regimes Shape Encoding (Claims 69-81)**
The specific encoding techniques are responses to specific legal and social environments. Different regimes (decriminalization with social persecution, explicit criminalization, state surveillance, internal community policing) produce different encoding strategies. The writer must understand their constraint field.

**5. Translation and Reception as Additional Encoding Layers (Claims 95-113)**
The encoding does not stop at the original text. Translators, editors, critics, and publishers add layers of encoding or decoding. The reception history of each author shows how critical frameworks can institutionalize the concealment.

**6. Intersectionality Compounds the Problem (Claims 137, 138, 140, 141, 165)**
Writers managing multiple stigmatized identities (Baldwin: race + sexuality; Delany: race + sexuality + genre; Bishop: gender + sexuality) face qualitatively different encoding problems that single-axis models do not fully capture.

**7. The Schema Has Limits and Breaking Points (Claims 5, 85, 86, 88, 137)**
The encoding system can be pushed to collapse (Delany), silence (Rimbaud), imprisonment (Wilde), or destruction (Burroughs's misogyny, the Vollmer killing). The schema documents these limits honestly.
