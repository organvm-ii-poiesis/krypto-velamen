## Key Concepts Glossary

*   **Stonewall Uprising (1969):** A spontaneous rebellion against a police raid at NYC’s Stonewall Inn  [pbs.org](https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/americanexperience/films/stonewall/#:~:text=When%20police%20raided%20the%20Stonewall,States%20and%20around%20the%20world)  . It _“symbolized the start of the modern… LGBTQ movement”_  [outhistory.org]()  . In QV33R, Stonewall is a foundational event; its symbols (e.g. the rainbow flag) and slogans recur as signifiers of resistance.
    
*   **Gay Liberation:** A 1960s–70s radical movement for LGBTQ rights after Stonewall. It emphasized visibility and rights rather than shameful silence  [news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2019/06/harvard-scholars-reflect-on-the-history-and-legacy-of-the-stonewall-riots/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CIt%20really%20is%20like%20the,War%20and%20civil%20rights%20and)  . Relevant to QV33R as the era that forged activist tactics and queer solidarity.
    
*   **Queer Spaces:** Physical or virtual locations created or reclaimed by LGBTQ+ people—bars, clubs, community centers, parks, websites. Gay bars long served as informal “third places” where patrons found community and support  [humanities.uci.edu](https://www.humanities.uci.edu/news/legacy-lgbtq-nightlife#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20gay%20bar%20as%20an,%E2%80%9D)  . Queer neighborhoods (“gayborhoods”) form when many LGBTQ+ people cluster in one urban area  [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7978650/#:~:text=propose%20macro,Forstie%20%2048%3B%20Ghaziani%202015a)  . QV33R explores how these spaces evolved (e.g. many gay bars declined with apps  [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7978650/#:~:text=researchers%20find%20that%20these%20so,Rosser%20et%20al)  , but new spaces emerge).
    
*   **Safe Space:** A venue (classroom, organization, café) explicitly welcoming LGBTQ+ people. Originated in the 1980s–90s to signal non-hostile environment. In QV33R, any narrative setting (e.g. the queer archive itself) may be treated as a “safe space” symbolically.
    
*   **Chosen/Found Family:** Non-biological kin groups formed by LGBTQ+ individuals for emotional and material support when biological families reject them  [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8066340/#:~:text=to%20disowning%20as%20a%20result,4)    [savie-lgbtq.uqam.ca](https://savie-lgbtq.uqam.ca/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Chosen_families_social_networks.pdf#:~:text=Chosen%20Families%20A%20chosen%20family,which%20are%20translated%2C%20among%20other)  . For example, friends sharing housing or chosen siblings raising each other’s children. In QV33R, chosen families exemplify community resilience and alternative kinship.
    
*   **Intersectionality:** A framework (coined 1989) describing how multiple identities (race, gender, class, sexuality) interact to produce distinct experiences of oppression  [scholarship.law.columbia.edu](https://scholarship.law.columbia.edu/faculty_scholarship/3007/#:~:text=White%3B%20All%20the%20Blacks%20Are,feminist%20theory%20and%20antiracist%20politics)  . For QV33R, intersectionality means e.g. telling queer history inclusive of women, BIPOC, disabled queer voices, not only white gay men.
    
*   **Queer-of-Color Critique:** Perspectives from queer people of color (QPOC) who critique mainstream (often white-dominated) LGBT narratives. For instance, activists like Marsha P. Johnson highlighted that early gay movements marginalized trans and non-white members  [womenshistory.org](https://www.womenshistory.org/education-resources/biographies/marsha-p-johnson#:~:text=more%20moderate%20and%20focused%20spin,their%20youth%20experiencing%20homelessness%20and)  . In QV33R, this concept guides more inclusive storytelling that acknowledges how racism and queerphobia have overlapped.
    
*   **Homonormativity:** A concept (Duggan 2003) criticizing a depoliticized, assimilationist gay culture that emphasizes marriage, monogamy, and consumption. E.g. portraying wealthy white gay couples as “normal” LGBT life. In QV33R, homonormativity may be queried or critiqued through alternative world-making.
    
*   **Queer Utopia (Utopianism):** Imagined LGBTQ+-friendly societies or futures, often embodied in art, literature, or political theory (e.g. Eva Gore-Booth’s early 20th-century journal _Urania_ aimed at “abolish[ing] gender binaries”  [news.vcu.edu](https://news.vcu.edu/article/2024/10/how-i-found-my-research-moira-neve-studies-visions-of-a-queer-utopia#:~:text=I%20want%20to%20bring%20contemporary,a%20desire%20for%20social%20improvement)  ). José Muñoz described _“queerness as utopian feeling”_, an aspirational horizon for social justice. In QV33R, utopian concepts might underpin the archive’s narrative design or envisioned future worlds for queers.
    
*   **Ballroom Culture / Houses:** Underground drag ballroom competitions (originating in New York in the 1970s–80s) organized by _“houses”_ that functioned as chosen families. Documented in the film _Paris Is Burning_, they exemplify creativity and kinship in marginalized queer communities. As a case study, they illustrate chosen-family dynamics in QV33R’s context.
    
*   **Safe Harbor Community (STAR):** An early example (founded 1970) of an outreach organization by trans activists (Johnson/Rivera) to shelter homeless queer youth of color  [womenshistory.org](https://www.womenshistory.org/education-resources/biographies/marsha-p-johnson#:~:text=LGBTQ%2B%20people%20of%20color%20from,their%20youth%20experiencing%20homelessness%20and)  . It blurs the line between chosen family and social service. In QV33R, it’s a historical touchstone for world-building around queer sanctuary.
    

(_Each concept above is cited or inspired by the sources listed, grounding QV33R’s glossary in documented definitions._)

## Case Study Stack

**Case 1 – Stonewall Uprising (NYC, June 1969):** _What happened:_ Patrons of the Stonewall Inn resisted arrest during a police raid, sparking a six-day series of protests in Greenwich Village. This urban rebellion united gay men, lesbians, drag queens and others  [outhistory.org]()    [education.nationalgeographic.org](https://education.nationalgeographic.org/resource/how-stonewall-uprising-ignited-modern-lgbtq-rights-movement/#:~:text=pour%20out%2C%20lining%20up%20on,so%20police%20could%20arrest%20them)  . _How represented:_ Many histories (e.g. Carter’s _Stonewall_ book), documentaries (e.g. PBS _American Experience_), films, and Pride commemorations depict Stonewall. It’s often mythologized with focal figures (e.g. Marsha P. Johnson) though accounts vary  [education.nationalgeographic.org](https://education.nationalgeographic.org/resource/how-stonewall-uprising-ignited-modern-lgbtq-rights-movement/#:~:text=One%20person%20fighting%20for%20her,melee%20was%20in%20full%20swing)    [news.harvard.edu](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2019/06/harvard-scholars-reflect-on-the-history-and-legacy-of-the-stonewall-riots/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CIt%20really%20is%20like%20the,War%20and%20civil%20rights%20and)  . _What it teaches QV33R:_ Stonewall teaches how spontaneous collective action can catalyze a movement. It also shows the importance of acknowledging all participants (e.g. trans and people of color were key  [womenshistory.org](https://www.womenshistory.org/education-resources/biographies/marsha-p-johnson#:~:text=LGBTQ%2B%20people%20of%20color%20from,their%20youth%20experiencing%20homelessness%20and)  ). In QV33R, Stonewall’s legacy likely serves as an origin story, teaching that space and crisis can trigger world-making.

**Case 2 – First Pride March (NYC, June 1970):** _What happened:_ On the first anniversary of Stonewall, activists held the Christopher Street Liberation Day parade  [education.nationalgeographic.org](https://education.nationalgeographic.org/resource/how-stonewall-uprising-ignited-modern-lgbtq-rights-movement/#:~:text=In%201970%2C%20a%20year%20after,been%20widely%20recognized%20for%20their)  . Approximately 5,000 people marched demanding equality. _How represented:_ This event is remembered as the first gay pride parade, often depicted in media reports and histories as the birth of annual Pride celebrations  [education.nationalgeographic.org](https://education.nationalgeographic.org/resource/how-stonewall-uprising-ignited-modern-lgbtq-rights-movement/#:~:text=In%201970%2C%20a%20year%20after,been%20widely%20recognized%20for%20their)    [academic.oup.com](https://academic.oup.com/hwj/article-pdf/doi/10.1093/hwj/dbz051/32906158/dbz051.pdf#:~:text=,Parade%E2%80%99%20%E2%80%93%20in%20which%20LGBT%C3%BE)  . _What it teaches QV33R:_ Pride shows how commemoration becomes movement. It illustrates the shift from rebellion to organized visibility. It also highlights the role of queer public celebration as world-making – turning a riot into a ritual. For QV33R, Pride exemplifies how community rituals can preserve history and imagine joy.

**Case 3 – Ballroom Culture and Houses (NYC, 1970s–90s):** _What happened:_ In NYC’s Black and Latinx queer communities, underground drag balls were held where “houses” competed in fashion and dance. Abandoned by family and society, members formed chosen-family houses (e.g. House of LaBeija) as surrogate kin. _How represented:_ The 1990 film _Paris Is Burning_ documents this scene, as do later documentaries and queer histories. Houses are portrayed as intense chosen-families, with “mothers” and “children”  [womenshistory.org](https://www.womenshistory.org/education-resources/biographies/marsha-p-johnson#:~:text=LGBTQ%2B%20people%20of%20color%20from,their%20youth%20experiencing%20homelessness%20and)  . _What it teaches QV33R:_ This case highlights chosen-family world-making under oppression: a joyous, defiant subculture creating home and identity. It also exemplifies intersection (race, class, gender) at the margins. QV33R can learn from how these communities built their own world of norms and values when mainstream society excluded them.

**Case 4 – Combahee River Collective Statement (Boston, 1977):** _What happened:_ A group of Black lesbian feminists (including Barbara Smith, Audre Lorde, etc.) issued the Combahee River Collective Statement, articulating the intersections of race, gender, class, and sexuality in their lives. _How represented:_ This manifesto is a seminal document in both queer and feminist scholarship and is frequently cited in academic literature on intersectionality. It critiqued both white feminist and mainstream gay organizations for neglecting Black queer women. _What it teaches QV33R:_ The Combahee case teaches the importance of centering the experiences of queer people of color. It introduces the language and logic of intersectional critique, suggesting that any queer world-making must address multiple oppressions. For QV33R, it underscores that in building worlds, all voices and intersections must be included or the vision is incomplete.

How did Stonewall-like events occur in Africa, Asia, or Latin America, and what local narratives exist? _Next:_ Seek archives of non-Western LGBT movements (e.g. early Bangkok or India activism) and scholarship on them.

## Ethical Handling of Sensitive Topics

*   **Privacy and Outing:** No real person’s private information should be disclosed. We cite only public figures or anonymized roles (e.g. “patron” or “activist”). For living individuals (especially non-famous queer people), we avoid naming or speculating. Archives should be cited without extracting identifying details not already public.
    
*   **Avoiding Stereotypes:** We describe groups and events factually, avoiding caricatures of gender or race. For instance, we cite that Johnson was Black and trans, but focus on her activism rather than any stereotype. We do not generalize (“all gay people do X”) and we contextualize behaviors historically.
    
*   **Harassment and Hate Speech:** In quoting primary sources (e.g. 1969 police reports, activist language), any derogatory terms are cited in context and clearly attributed as part of historical record. We do not use slurs or hateful language except in block quotes that are properly contextualized and necessary.
    
*   **Representation and Consent:** When using oral histories or interviews (e.g. Mark Segal’s quote  [education.nationalgeographic.org](https://education.nationalgeographic.org/resource/how-stonewall-uprising-ignited-modern-lgbtq-rights-movement/#:~:text=In%20an%20oral%20history%2C%20activist,for%20their%20rights%2C%20what%20about)  ), we note these are public, published statements. We do not present an archival diary or interview excerpt as direct evidence unless it’s published or in a citable archive.
    
*   **Cultural Sensitivity:** We present intersectional critique and histories of marginalized groups with care. For example, discussing “transvestite” language in 1970s slang, we quote historical terms in context and use modern respectful terms (“transgender woman” etc.) for clarity. We acknowledge any outdated terms as products of their time, not as endorsements.
